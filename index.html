What is Stoichiometry?

Stoichiometry is the branch of chemistry that deals with the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions. It's based on the law of conservation of mass and allows chemists to predict how much product will form or how much reactant is needed.

Key Concepts:

  • Balanced Chemical Equations: Show the exact number of atoms of each element on both sides
  • Mole Ratios: Derived from coefficients in balanced equations
  • Limiting Reagent: The reactant that runs out first, limiting product formation
  • Theoretical Yield: Maximum amount of product possible from given reactants

Example Calculation:

Problem: How many grams of water can be produced from 10g of hydrogen and 80g of oxygen?

Balanced equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

  1. Convert masses to moles: H₂ = 10g ÷ 2g/mol = 5 mol; O₂ = 80g ÷ 32g/mol = 2.5 mol
  2. Determine limiting reagent: Need 2:1 ratio. Have 5:2.5 = 2:1 ratio (neither limiting)
  3. Calculate product: 5 mol H₂ × (2 mol H₂O / 2 mol H₂) × 18g/mol = 90g H₂O

Frequently Asked Questions

Stoichiometry is the calculation of quantities in chemical reactions based on the balanced chemical equation. It allows you to determine how much of each reactant is needed and how much product will be formed. The word comes from Greek: "stoicheion" (element) and "metron" (measure).

To balance chemical equations, adjust the coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) so that the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides of the equation. Start with the most complex molecule and work systematically through each element. Never change the chemical formulas themselves, only the coefficients.

A limiting reagent (or limiting reactant) is the reactant that is completely consumed first in a chemical reaction, thus limiting the amount of product that can be formed. It determines the theoretical yield of the reaction. The other reactants are present in excess.

Theoretical yield is calculated by: 1) Determining the limiting reagent, 2) Using stoichiometry to calculate the maximum amount of product that could be formed based on the balanced equation and the amount of limiting reagent available. This assumes 100% efficiency and no side reactions.
